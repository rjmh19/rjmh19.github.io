Phase 1 · Anatomical adaptation · 6 weeks · Centr 3
Goal is movement pattern quality, not load. Tendons, connective tissue, and joint structures adapt more slowly than muscle — this phase builds the foundation they need. Load should feel almost too easy for the first 3–4 weeks. That is correct. Three sessions per week on Monday, Wednesday, Friday. Never strength train on back-to-back days.
Warm-up — 5 min
W
Concept 2 row — easy pace
warm-up · full body activation
5 min
full body
Damper setting 3–4. Rate 18–20 strokes per minute. Relaxed pace — this is activation, not cardio. Focus on the sequence: legs push → lean back → arms pull. Reverse to return. The row warms the hips, posterior chain, upper back, and core simultaneously — ideal pre-strength activation. Keep the grip loose throughout. Left wrist stays neutral — do not let it flex backward at the catch. If the catch position causes wrist discomfort, use an overhand grip with a slightly shorter reach.
Keep left wrist neutral at catch — do not flex backward
Lower body — hip, glute, quad, calf
1
Cable pull-through
low cable · hip hinge pattern
2 × 12
glutes / hips
Set the low cable with the rope handle. Stand facing away from the machine, straddle the cable. Hinge at the hips — push them back, soft knee bend, chest stays up. Drive hips forward to stand tall. Squeeze glutes at the top. 12 slow reps. This teaches the hip hinge — the foundational movement for glute loading, deadlifts, and posterior chain work. Start very light (5–10kg) — the goal is pattern, not load. Bone density benefit from hip loading begins here.
Rope handle — neutral grip, wrist safe throughout bone density
2
Goblet squat — Smith bar or cable
smith bar / low cable · squat pattern
2 × 10
quads / glutes
Use the Smith bar at chest height or hold the rope at chest from the low cable. Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out. Sit back and down — knees track over toes, chest stays up, depth to comfortable range only. Drive through the whole foot to stand. Do not let the knees cave inward. Start with bodyweight or minimal load (the bar only). The squat pattern is the most important lower body movement for skiing and functional daily life. Depth improves over weeks — do not force it early.
Hands cupped around bar — no wrist loading required bone density
3
Reverse lunge — bodyweight
bodyweight · single leg pattern
2 × 8 each
quads / glutes
Stand tall, hands on hips or arms forward for balance. Step one foot back — lower the back knee toward the floor, front shin stays vertical. Push through the front heel to return. 8 each leg, alternating. Reverse lunge is safer than forward lunge for knee tracking and easier to control. Single-leg work is critical for skiing balance and gait mechanics. Do not hold cables or weights until the pattern is solid — typically 2–3 sessions. If balance is unstable, hold the Smith bar lightly with one hand.
Hands on hips or lightly touch Smith bar — no grip load
4
Standing calf raise — Smith bar
smith bar · bilateral
2 × 15
calves / ankles
Stand on the floor or on a step if available, Smith bar resting lightly across upper traps (or hands on bar for balance only). Rise slowly onto the balls of both feet, pause 1–2 seconds at the top, lower with control over 3 seconds. The slow lowering (eccentric) phase is where the real adaptation happens — do not rush down. Calf strength supports running economy, ankle stability, and gout joint management. 15 reps is correct at this stage — higher rep range for calves builds endurance capacity first.
Hands resting on bar only — no downward wrist pressure
Upper body — pull and push
5
Cable lat pulldown — curl bar
high cable · bilateral pull
2 × 12
lats / rhomboids
Attach the curl bar to the high cable. Sit or stand, grip the bar with a shoulder-width underhand or neutral grip. Pull the bar to upper chest — lead with the elbows, not the hands. Pause at the bottom. Slow return over 3 seconds. Start light enough that you feel the lats working, not the biceps. The left rhomboid tightness means the left shoulder blade may not retract as fully as the right — this is fine, do not force symmetry. Over weeks, the pulling motion will gently improve the rhomboid range.
Underhand grip keeps wrist neutral — avoid overhand if it causes wrist strain
6
Seated cable row — D-handles
low cable · bilateral pull
2 × 12
mid-back / rhomboids
Sit on the bench facing the low cable, D-handles in both hands, palms facing each other (neutral grip). Sit tall, slight lean back from the hip. Pull handles to lower ribcage — squeeze shoulder blades together at the end. Slow 3-second return. Neutral grip is completely wrist-safe. This is the primary upper back rebuilding exercise — the rhomboids and mid-traps are the postural muscles most lost to desk life and reduced activity. The left rhomboid tightness may feel the stretch more on this side — that is therapeutic.
Neutral grip D-handles — palms facing each other, wrist fully safe
7
Cable chest press — D-handles
mid cable · bilateral push
2 × 12
chest / shoulders
Set both cables at mid-chest height. Stand facing away from the machine, D-handle in each hand, palms facing down or slightly inward. Step forward slightly. Press both handles forward to full arm extension — do not lock elbows. Slow 3-second return, feel the chest stretch at the end. This replaces the push-up, which would cause backward wrist pressure. The cable provides constant tension throughout the range. Keep the wrists straight — do not let them bend backward at any point during the press.
Wrists straight throughout — cable press replaces push-ups for wrist safety
8
Cable shoulder press — D-handles
low cable · overhead push
2 × 10
shoulders / traps
Set cables low. Stand, D-handles at shoulder height, palms forward. Press both arms overhead to full extension. Lower with control over 3 seconds. Start very light — overhead pressing is a demanding pattern and the shoulder stabilisers need time to adapt. Stop well below the point of any impingement or discomfort. The wrist stays in a neutral-to-slight-extension position that is safe for the left thumb — avoid loading in full wrist flexion. If any left thumb sharp pain: stop and substitute lateral raises.
Keep wrists in neutral position — stop if left thumb sharp pain occurs
Core — stability and anti-rotation
9
Dead bug
floor · supine · no wrist load
2 × 8 each
core / stability
Lie on your back, arms pointing to ceiling, knees bent at 90 degrees in the air. Slowly lower one arm overhead and the opposite leg toward the floor simultaneously — lower back stays flat on the floor. Return. Alternate. 8 per side. Do not let the lower back arch away from the floor — this is the entire exercise. The dead bug trains the deep core stabilisers (transverse abdominis) in a completely wrist-safe position. It is the most important core exercise for running form, injury prevention, and protecting the lumbar spine during skiing.
Completely wrist-safe — arms move freely with no loading
10
Cable Pallof press
mid cable · anti-rotation
2 × 10 each
core / anti-rotation
Set one cable at chest height. Stand sideways to the machine, D-handle held at chest with both hands. Press the handle straight out from your chest — hold 2 seconds, return. The core resists the cable pulling you toward the machine. 10 reps, then turn and repeat on the other side. Very light weight — 5–8kg. The Pallof press is anti-rotation core work — it trains the obliques and deep core to resist twisting forces, which is exactly what running, skiing, and paddling demand. Wrist stays neutral holding the D-handle.
D-handle, neutral grip — light load, wrist safe
Mobility complement — post-session · 5 min
S1
Hip flexor kneeling stretch
kneeling · post-squat and lunge
60s each
hip flexors
Kneel on one knee, other foot forward. Shift weight forward gently until you feel the stretch at the front of the back hip. Hold 60 seconds. Breathe. Hip flexors tighten with running and sitting — this directly counters that. Critical for lower back health and running mechanics. Switch sides. Do not force range — a gentle sustained stretch is more effective and safer than an aggressive one. Forearm on front knee for support if needed.
Forearm on knee for support — wrist unloaded
S2
Doorway or cable chest opener
standing · post-press
45s each
chest / shoulders
Set one cable to shoulder height with a D-handle. Hold the handle, step forward until you feel the stretch across the chest and front of the shoulder. Turn slightly away, hold 45 seconds. Switch sides. Alternatively use a doorway — arm at 90 degrees, lean through. Counters the forward shoulder posture that pressing work can reinforce if not balanced. Especially valuable for paddling shoulder health.
Hold cable with relaxed hand — no grip effort required
S3
Seated piriformis stretch
bench · post-lower body
60s each
glutes / hips
Sit on the bench, cross one ankle over the opposite knee (figure-4 position). Sit tall and gently lean forward from the hips until you feel the stretch deep in the glute. Hold 60 seconds. Switch sides. The piriformis and deep hip rotators are heavily loaded during squats, lunges, and running. This stretch directly addresses the hip tightness that feeds into lower back and IT band issues. Hands resting on the shin — no wrist loading.
Hands resting on shin — no wrist load required
Phase 1 principle
Movement quality over load. Always. In the anatomical adaptation phase, the nervous system is learning patterns and connective tissue is adapting. Adding load too early interrupts both processes. If a set feels easy, that is correct. The load increases in Phase 3. The pattern you build now is the one you will use for years.
Phase 2 · Cottage · Summer · Dumbbells + rucking + canoe
Goal is to maintain the movement patterns and adaptation built in Phase 1. Equipment is limited but sufficient — dumbbells for structured sessions, rucking as the load-bearing cardio complement, canoe and wood chopping as functional training. The canoe and outdoor activity count as real training load — on heavy paddling or rucking days, reduce session volume by one set per exercise. Three sessions per week, M/W/F pattern maintained. Never on back-to-back days.
Lower body
1
Dumbbell goblet squat
dumbbell · squat pattern
2–3 × 12
quads / glutes
Hold one dumbbell vertically at chest height, both hands cupped around one end. Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out. Squat to comfortable depth, drive through the whole foot to stand. Slow and controlled. By Phase 2 your squat pattern will be established — start adding load gradually (increase 2kg when 12 reps feel easy across two sessions). The cupped grip is completely wrist-safe for the left hand.
Both hands cupped around dumbbell end — no wrist strain bone density
2
Dumbbell Romanian deadlift
dumbbells · hip hinge
2–3 × 10
glutes / hamstrings
Hold a dumbbell in each hand, palms facing thighs. Hinge at the hips — push them back, lower the dumbbells along the front of the legs to mid-shin, feel the hamstring stretch, drive hips forward to stand. Squeeze glutes at top. The hip hinge pattern from Phase 1 pull-throughs transfers directly here. Keep the dumbbells close to the legs throughout. Left grip: hold the dumbbell with a relaxed grip — the weight is supported by the fingers, not gripped with the thumb.
Relaxed grip — weight rests in fingers, minimal thumb pressure bone density
3
Reverse lunge — dumbbells
dumbbells · single leg
2–3 × 8 each
quads / glutes
Hold light dumbbells at sides. Step back, lower back knee toward ground, drive through front heel to return. By Phase 2 the pattern should be solid — add light load (4–6kg) if balance is stable. Dumbbells hang at the side — the left hand only needs to support the weight against gravity, no backward wrist pressure involved.
Dumbbells hang at side — neutral wrist position
4
Single-leg calf raise
bodyweight · one leg
2 × 12 each
calves
Stand on one foot, hand lightly on a wall or tree for balance. Rise onto the ball of the foot, pause 2 seconds, lower slowly over 3–4 seconds. 12 each side. Single-leg version is a significant load increase from bilateral — it may feel challenging with bodyweight alone. The slow lowering is essential. Progress to holding a light dumbbell when 12 reps feel easy.
Touch wall for balance only — no grip load
Upper body
5
Rucking — loaded pack carry
outdoors · posterior chain + cardio
30–45 min
full body
Use your existing pack loaded to 20–25 lbs. Walk at a brisk pace on trails or varied terrain. Maintain upright posture — do not lean forward into the pack. Shoulders back, core engaged throughout. Rucking loads the posterior chain, builds bone density through axial loading, and provides cardiovascular stimulus without the joint impact of running. On heavy ruck days (45+ min), count it as a training session and reduce the strength session to lower body only. Completely wrist-safe — pack straps across shoulders, hands free.
Completely wrist-safe — hands free throughout bone density — axial load

Dumbbell bent-over row
dumbbell · unilateral pull
2 × 10 each
lats / mid-back
Hinge forward at the hips, one hand resting on a surface for support (bench, chair, rock). Hold dumbbell in the other hand hanging straight down. Pull the dumbbell to the lower ribcage — elbow drives up and back. Lower with control. 10 reps, switch sides. Left arm row: hold the dumbbell in a loose grip, supported by the fingers. The thumb does not need to grip tightly. Right arm rows can use full grip strength as normal.
Left hand: loose grip, weight supported by fingers — thumb relaxed

Dumbbell shoulder press
dumbbells · overhead push
2 × 10
shoulders
Seated or standing. Dumbbells at shoulder height, palms facing forward or slightly inward. Press overhead to full extension without locking elbows. Lower with control — 3 seconds down. Keep wrists straight — do not let them bend backward. Left hand: use a weight where the thumb is not under pressure. If there is any thumb discomfort, reduce load until it resolves. Start light — the shoulder stabilisers are still adapting.

floor · core stability
2 × 10 each
core
Same as Phase 1 but increase reps to 10 per side. If this is easy, hold a light dumbbell in each hand pointing to the ceiling — this adds load to the core as the arm moves overhead. Lower back must stay flat. This is the cottage core staple — no equipment needed beyond a mat or flat ground.

9
Suitcase carry — dumbbell
dumbbell · loaded carry
2 × 30m each
core / anti-lateral
Hold one dumbbell at your side. Walk 30 metres tall and upright — do not lean toward or away from the weight. Switch hands, return. The suitcase carry is one of the most functional core exercises — it trains the core to resist lateral flexion under load, which is exactly what paddling and carrying gear requires. Right hand at full load, left hand at reduced load (or skip left if thumb discomfort). Walk on any terrain — uneven ground adds to the stability demand.
Right hand full load · left hand reduced load or omit if thumb discomfort
Phase 2 principle
The canoe is training. The ruck is training. The wood chopping is training. Paddling loads the lats, rhomboids, core, and shoulders in exactly the functional pattern this program is building. Rucking builds bone density and posterior chain endurance without joint impact. Wood chopping develops rotational power. On heavy outdoor days, reduce the structured session to lower body only and core. The cottage summer is not a break from the program — it is the program in its natural environment.
Phase 3 · Centr 3 return · Fall/Winter · Strength endurance + progressive load
By now the movement patterns are solid, connective tissue has adapted, and body composition has changed. Phase 3 introduces progressive load increases on the compound movements and begins building genuine strength endurance. Ski season prep from October. Bone density work intensifies with heavier compound loading.

The goblet squat graduates to a Smith bar back squat at this stage if form is solid. Cable pull-through transitions toward a single-leg Romanian deadlift for continued hip and glute development. Bone density stimulus increases with heavier compound loading — this is the phase where that benefit becomes significant.

Ski prep — October focus
Centr 3 · sport specific
2 × 12
quads / lateral
From October, add two ski-specific exercises: Cable lateral squat (squat while moving laterally — mimics traverse and mogul skiing) and Cable lateral band walk (ankle strap, low cable, walk sideways — glute medius, the key ski stabiliser). Both are wrist-safe. Wall sit (60 seconds, build to 90) is added as a finisher — it directly builds the sustained quad endurance skiing demands. These supplement the base program, not replace it.

Phase 3 principle
Load is a tool, not a goal. Increase weight to maintain the stimulus as the body adapts — but the movement pattern always governs the load, not the other way around. If form degrades under load, reduce the weight. The bone density and body composition goals are both served by consistent progressive loading over months and years, not by chasing numbers in any single session.
Wrist and thumb protocols
General rule: No backward wrist pressure. No push-up position. No heavy gripping with the left thumb. Cable and dumbbell work with neutral grip is the default. If any exercise causes sharp left thumb pain — stop immediately, substitute or omit. Background soreness is acceptable; sharp pain is a signal.
Grip strategy: Right hand grips normally. Left hand uses a supported or relaxed grip — the weight rests in the fingers, the thumb acts as a guide only. Straps can be used on pulling exercises if grip fatigue becomes limiting.
Pressing substitution: All push-up variations are permanently replaced with cable chest press. This is not a temporary modification — it is the correct exercise for this anatomy.
Stiffness protocols
General stiffness (≤4/10): Proceed as planned. Extend warm-up to 7 minutes on the rower. Reduce load by 20% on lower body if hip stiffness is present. Proceed normally on upper body and core.
High stiffness (>4/10): Mobility session only — no strength training. Running is also substituted per the fitness parking note. Do not force strength work through high stiffness — the joints need fluid movement, not load.
Gout flare: Strength training paused entirely during active flare. Resume with Phase 1 loads on return, regardless of which phase you were in. One week gradual return before resuming prior loads.
GLP-1 protocol
Protein priority: Target 1.6–2g protein per kg bodyweight daily (165–205g at current weight, scaling down as weight reduces). Prioritise protein at every meal. Resistance training without adequate protein on GLP-1 results in significant lean mass loss alongside fat.
Session on reduced appetite days: Do not skip strength sessions because appetite is suppressed. Have a small protein-containing snack before the session if necessary. The training stimulus is what protects muscle — consistency matters more than any single session feeling perfect.
Energy management: GLP-1 may reduce energy available for training, especially early. Reduce loads by 10–15% on low-energy days rather than skipping. Maintain frequency over intensity.
Recovery protocols
Monday / Wednesday / Friday: This is the session pattern. Running fits on Tuesday, Thursday, Saturday or Sunday. Never on back-to-back days. If a session is missed, do not double up — resume the next scheduled day.
Sleep — active HFD goal: Target 7 hours average and Garmin score 80+ consistently. Current baseline ~6 hours / score 70. Sleep improvement is the single highest-leverage recovery intervention available. Until sleep improves, do not increase volume beyond the M/W/F pattern. When in doubt, do less.

Load increase rule
The 2-session rule: Only increase load when the current weight feels comfortable and controlled across two consecutive sessions. Not one good day — two. Increase by the smallest available increment (typically 2.5–5kg on cables, 2kg on dumbbells). Never increase load and volume simultaneously.
